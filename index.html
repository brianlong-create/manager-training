Block 1

Tactical Management & Leadership

Core frameworks from Simon Sinek and Leila Hormozi. Watch, discuss, apply.

1. Start With Why

Simon Sinek • The Golden Circle
1

The Golden Circle: WHY (purpose) → HOW (process) → WHAT (product). Most organizations communicate outside-in. Great leaders communicate inside-out.

2

People don't buy what you do, they buy why you do it. This applies to your team too.

3

Your WHY is your filter. Use it to make decisions, hire, and say no to things that don't align.

Discussion Question

"What's the 'why' behind your team's work right now? Not the what — the why."

Framework · The Golden Circle

Facilitator Script

▶

Simon Sinek's Golden Circle has three rings. The outer ring is WHAT - for our team: We build and operate ecosystem demo environments for Motorola Solutions. The middle ring is HOW - through repeatable, scalable platforms that let sellers self-serve demos. The inner ring is WHY - we believe every seller deserves tools as good as the products they sell.

Most teams communicate WHAT first. Inspired leaders communicate WHY first. When people connect to the WHY, the WHAT becomes proof of purpose.

Key application: Next time you are in a team meeting, don't start with the sprint board. Start with WHY this sprint matters.

2. Leader vs Manager

Distinguishing the two roles
1

Managers handle complexity. Planning, budgeting, organizing. It's about order.

2

Leaders handle change. Setting direction, aligning people, motivating. It's about movement.

3

You need both. Know which hat you're wearing in each moment.

Paired Exercise (10 min)

Each manager lists 3 things they did last week. Categorize each as "managing" or "leading."

Group Debrief

"Where are you over-indexed on managing vs leading?"

Framework · Leader vs Manager

Facilitator Script

▶

Your title says manager but you do BOTH jobs. The Manager Function: budgets, timelines, staffing, process - creating ORDER. The Leader Function: vision, culture, trust, development, difficult conversations - creating MOVEMENT.

Motorola example: When a demo goes down during a customer presentation, the manager response is: file the ticket, assign the engineer, update the seller. The leader response is: take the heat publicly, protect your engineer, then run a blameless post-mortem.

Friday question: This week, was I mostly managing or mostly leading? Neither is wrong - but if the answer is always the same, something is off.

3. Circle of Safety

Simon Sinek • Building Trust
1

Leaders set the circle. When people feel safe, they focus outward on threats and opportunities.

2

Trust is earned in drops and lost in buckets. Every interaction builds or erodes the circle.

3

Vulnerability is the entry fee. You go first. Model ownership and honesty about mistakes.

Vulnerable Discussion (8 min)

"Name a moment you broke the circle of safety on your team. What did you do about it?"

Framework · Circle of Safety

Facilitator Script

▶

When people feel safe, they focus energy OUTWARD on threats and opportunities. When unsafe, they focus INWARD on politics and self-protection.

A healthy circle: Mistakes discussed not weaponized. Disagreement welcomed not punished. Bad news travels FAST. People ask for help instead of hiding problems. Credit shared, blame absorbed by the leader.

Eco Demo reality: Our team sits between engineering, sales, and product. When a demo fails, someone wants to blame. Your job: absorb that shot and turn it into a learning conversation. When a seller is angry, YOU respond - not your engineer. Your people are watching.

4. Skill × Will Matrix

Assessing & developing your team
1

High Skill / High Will: Delegate and empower. Don't micromanage your stars.

2

High Skill / Low Will: Motivate and re-engage. Often a leadership problem.

3

Low Skill / High Will: Guide and train. Diamonds in the rough. Invest heavily.

4

Low Skill / Low Will: Direct conversation. Clear expectations, timelines, consequences.

Private Exercise (10 min)

Plot your direct reports on the matrix. Then discuss: "What's your move for each quadrant?"

Framework · Skill × Will Matrix

Facilitator Script

▶

Q1 Stars (High Skill/High Will): Delegate authority, not just tasks. Give ownership of outcomes. Ask what THEY want to learn next.

Q2 Disengaged Expert (High Skill/Low Will): Often a leadership failure. Have a genuine 1:1: "I have noticed a shift. What is true for you right now?"

Q3 Enthusiastic Beginner (Low Skill/High Will): Heavy 1:1 cadence. Pair with a Q1 star. Celebrate small wins LOUDLY - will is fragile early.

Q4 Hard Conversation (Low Skill/Low Will): Direct, documented conversation. Specific gaps, bounded timeline, clear consequences. Not a threat - a contract.

5. Radical Accountability

Owning outcomes, not excuses
1

Accountability starts at the top. If your team missed the target, YOU missed it.

2

The opposite of accountability is blame. Blame travels down, accountability travels up.

3

Public praise, private correction. Celebrate wins loudly. Address misses one-on-one.

Discussion Question

"What's a recent miss where you took accountability vs delegated blame?"

Framework · Radical Accountability Flow

Facilitator Script

▶

Common deflections to watch for: "The team missed it" (YOU missed it). "I told them to" (Did you verify? Follow up?). "Not my department" (If it affects your team, it is your problem).

The script: "This happened on my team. Here is what I have already done. Here is what I am going to do next. Here is when I will update you." No excuses, no backstory, no blame. Just ownership + action + timeline.

6. The $100/Hour Test

Leila Hormozi • Time Management for Leaders
1

Score every task: $10/hr, $100/hr, $1,000/hr, $10,000/hr.

2

$10/hr: Admin, data entry, scheduling. DELEGATE IT.

3

$100/hr: Problem solving, team coaching, process improvement. Your core job.

4

$1,000+/hr: Strategy, relationship building, innovation. This is where leaders win.

Exercise (10 min)

List your last 5 work activities. Score each. Identify what to delegate this week.

Facilitator Script

▶

$10/hr work: Email, meetings you don't need, reformatting slides. Needs doing - but not by YOU.

$100/hr: Problem solving, 1:1s, sprint planning. Your core management job.

$1,000/hr: Setting priorities, hiring decisions, fixing broken processes, hard performance conversations.

$10,000/hr: Strategic direction, key hires, killing bad projects, stakeholder relationships that unlock resources.

The trap: $10 work feels GOOD - it closes loops, generates dopamine. $1,000+ work feels BAD - slow, ambiguous. But that is where the leverage is. Look at last week's calendar. If more than 20% was $10 work, you have a delegation problem.

7. The Infinite Game

Simon Sinek • Long-term thinking
1

Finite games have winners and losers. Business is infinite — there's no final "win."

2

Play to outlast, not to beat. Build something that sustains.

3

Worthy rivals, not competitors. Learn from others who do things well.

Discussion Question

"Where are we playing a finite game when we should be playing infinite?"

Facilitator Script

▶

Finite games have known players, fixed rules, and endpoints. Infinite games have unknown players, changeable rules, no endpoint. Business is infinite.

Finite players: Optimize for short-term metrics. Sacrifice trust for results. Treat people as resources.

Infinite players: Build teams that outlast individuals. Invest in people even when expensive. Choose worthy rivals over enemies.

Eco Demo application: We are not trying to "win" demos. We are building a platform and team that makes Motorola sellers more effective for the next decade. Every shortcut - skipping docs, burning out engineers, shipping barely-working demos - is a finite move in an infinite game.

8. Closing Thoughts

Execution • Final inspiration

30-Day Action Plan

▶

Commitment 1: One leadership behavior to START. Not "be better" - specific: "I will start every 1:1 asking about development, not tasks."

Commitment 2: One $10/hr task to delegate. Name it. Name who. Name when.

Commitment 3: One hard conversation to have. You have been putting it off. Write who, what, and when - this week.

Accountability: Pair up. Exchange commitments. Check in at 15 and 30 days. Email your plan to yourself AND your partner before leaving.

HR Buzzwords — Click to Reveal

At-Will Employment
Click to reveal
Either party can end the relationship at any time, for any legal reason. Doesn't mean you can fire for illegal reasons.
Example: A new hire is dismissed after 60 days because the role didn't work out — no notice required, no cause needed (within legal limits).
Constructive Feedback
Click to reveal
Feedback delivered to help the recipient improve, focused on behavior and impact — not personality.
Example: Instead of "You're disorganized," say: "I noticed the last two demo handoffs were missing the equipment list. Let's build a checklist together."
Progressive Discipline
Click to reveal
Graduated steps before termination: verbal coaching → written warning → final warning → termination. Document every step.
Example: An employee repeatedly arrives late. Start with a conversation, then a written warning when it continues, then a final notice. Termination only follows if no change.
Hostile Work Environment
Click to reveal
Unwelcome conduct based on a protected characteristic, severe or pervasive enough to create an intimidating environment.
Example: A coworker repeatedly makes jokes about another's religion during meetings. Multiple complaints have been raised; the behavior continues — that's a hostile environment claim waiting to be filed.
Reasonable Accommodation
Click to reveal
Workplace adjustment that lets a qualified employee with a disability perform essential job functions. Interactive process with HR.
Example: An employee with a back injury asks for a standing desk and a modified shift to attend physical therapy. Both are typically reasonable accommodations.
Documentation
Click to reveal
Written records of conversations, performance issues, and decisions. The single most important manager habit — if it wasn't documented, it didn't happen.
Example: After a difficult performance conversation, you email a recap: "Following up on our talk today — we discussed X, you committed to Y, we'll check in on date Z." That email IS the documentation.
Performance Improvement Plan (PIP)
Click to reveal
Formal, time-bound plan listing specific behaviors or results an employee must meet to remain employed. Typically 30–90 days.
Example: An employee's quality has slipped for two months. The PIP says: "Achieve 90% defect-free deliveries over the next 60 days with weekly checkpoints. Failure to meet expectations may result in termination."
Protected Class
Click to reveal
Group legally protected from discrimination: race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age (40+), disability, pregnancy, genetic info, veteran status.
Example: You can't decline to promote someone because they're pregnant — pregnancy is protected. The decision must be based on documented performance, not status.
Retaliation
Click to reveal
Adverse action against an employee for engaging in legally protected activity (filing a complaint, reporting harassment, requesting leave). #1 EEOC charge.
Example: An employee files a harassment complaint. The next week their schedule changes to the worst shifts. Even if you say it's unrelated, that's a retaliation claim risk.
Disparate Impact
Click to reveal
A neutral policy that disproportionately affects a protected group. Intent doesn't matter — impact does.
Example: A new hiring test screens out 80% of female applicants but only 10% of males. Intent doesn't matter — the impact is discriminatory.
FMLA
Click to reveal
Up to 12 weeks unpaid, job-protected leave per year. Don't ask medical details — route to HR.
Example: An employee's parent is hospitalized. They take 4 weeks unpaid FMLA leave for care, then return to their same role. You cannot dock them for it or demote them.
Undue Hardship
Click to reveal
Legal threshold for denying an accommodation — significant difficulty or expense. High bar; most accommodations are low/no cost. Engage HR before denying.
Example: Most accommodations cost under $500 and require minimal disruption. Denying a $200 ergonomic chair as "undue hardship" won't fly — and EEOC will side with the employee.

Tabletop Scenarios — What Would You Do?

"You don't need to be HR — you need to know when to call HR."

⚠️ Scenario 1: The Complaint
A direct report tells you a peer made an off-color joke about their religion. What do you do?

The Right Move

Thank them. Don't dismiss or investigate yourself. Document what they said. Contact HR within 24 hours. Don't promise confidentiality you can't guarantee.

⚠️ Scenario 2: The Accommodation
A team member discloses ADHD and asks for flexible schedule. What's appropriate to ask?

The Right Move

CAN ask: what schedule changes help, essential functions, coverage. CANNOT ask: diagnosis details, medication, treatment. Route to HR for interactive process.

⚠️ Scenario 3: The Performance Issue
You've coached someone for 3 months with no improvement. What's next?

The Right Move

Move to formal documentation. Write specific gaps with examples. Contact HR about a PIP with measurable goals and clear timeline.

⚠️ Scenario 4: The "Off the Record"
A report says "Can I tell you something off the record?" before sharing concerns. How do you respond?

The Right Move

Be honest BEFORE they share: "I can't promise off the record if it involves harassment, discrimination, or safety." Then listen. Never promise what you can't deliver.

⚠️ Scenario 5: The Escalation Call
When do you loop in HR vs handle it yourself?

The Decision Framework

Handle yourself: Routine coaching, scheduling, workload, skill development.

Loop in HR: Discrimination, harassment, retaliation, ADA, FMLA, threats, legal claims, termination.

Rule of thumb: "When in doubt, document and call HR."

Honest Roundtable of Talk

Open conversation before we head out. Share feedback, realign expectations, ask for adjustments. Candor over comfort.

Framework · Three Lenses
Ground Rules

One voice at a time. Listen all the way through before responding. No defending, no deflecting — if feedback stings, breathe before you answer. What's said here stays here unless we agree to action it together.

Roundtable Prompts · pick what fits
  • What's been working on this team that we should keep doing?
  • What's been frustrating or wasteful that has to change?
  • What's one thing you wish Brian (or a peer) did differently?
  • What's an adjustment to your role, workload, or scope you'd ask for?
  • What haven't you said out loud yet that you want to say now?
Closing Pass

Each person says one thing they're taking away — a recommitment, a request, or a thank-you. Brian goes last.
